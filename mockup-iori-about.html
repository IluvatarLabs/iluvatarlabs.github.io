About the Iluvatar Open Research Initiative

A public open-science program where Marvin performs computational biology research on behalf of the scientific community — free, openly published, and independently reviewable.

Why this exists

Computational biology has a throughput problem. Public data is abundant but fragmented; labs often know what they'd ask of it but can't staff the integration work. IORI exists because we think a well-vetted automated scientist can close that gap — and because the most credible demonstration of that capability is a public trail of work that real scientists can inspect and challenge.

Public data is abundant but fragmented

Decades of scRNA-seq, perturb-seq, GWAS, proteomics, and methylation data sit across disconnected repositories. The evidence exists; the integration doesn't.

Most labs can't staff the computational work

Wet-lab PIs know what they'd ask of the data; most don't have the computational team to integrate it across modalities, especially for rare or underfunded disease areas.

Marvin can synthesize across evidence

Marvin searches the public scientific record, integrates multi-modal data, reasons over it, and produces testable hypotheses — with full logic trails covering every filter decision and parameter choice.

Open review turns hypotheses into science

Rigor comes from scrutiny. Wet-lab validation, dataset corrections, community critique, and sometimes quiet refutation are what separates signal from noise. IORI is structured to make that loop visible and accountable.

How it works

A six-step loop. Each project moves through these phases and then re-enters the loop as new evidence accumulates — projects are "living," not one-shot.

01 · Submit

A PI submits a biological question via the structured form — the question, known data sources, and what validation they could perform. Or Iluvatar selects a Showcase project internally.

02 · Select

A review committee (2–3 internal PhD scientists + 1–2 rotating external advisors) evaluates each quarterly cohort for tractability, impact, Marvin's comparative advantage, and portfolio diversity. Full criteria →

03 · Analyze

Marvin runs a multi-stage pipeline: literature synthesis, data retrieval, integration across modalities, differential analysis, network modeling, hypothesis generation. Every step is logged.

04 · Vet

Internal PhD scientists review every output before publication. Findings are framed as hypotheses with explicit confidence levels, not claims of truth. Confidence may go down between iterations — that's a feature.

05 · Publish

Preprint posted to bioRxiv. GitHub repo opened with the full pipeline, data manifest, analysis artifacts, and hypothesis file. Community is invited to review and contribute.

06 · Iterate

New datasets, community feedback, and emerging literature trigger re-analysis. Marvin's updates are proposed as pull requests — 7-day community review window, visible diffs, auditable history.

Two tracks

Track 1 · Showcase (Iluvatar-initiated)

Iluvatar selects 2–3 high-impact computational biology problems per period, runs them through Marvin end-to-end, vets the results, and publishes. Purpose: proof of capability under controlled conditions. Selection prioritizes rare or underfunded therapeutic areas, problems requiring fragmented-data integration, and high probability of non-obvious findings.

Track 2 · Community-submitted

External PIs submit their own biological questions via the structured form. Iluvatar selects a subset per cohort and delivers results to the PI and the public simultaneously. Purpose: network growth and word-of-mouth. This track centers the scientist's problem — not ours — which is why we expect it to be the larger engine over time.

Selection criteria

Each submission is evaluated against five weighted criteria. No hidden factors; the selection committee's reasoning is published alongside each cohort announcement.

Scientific tractability · highest weight

Is there enough relevant public data for meaningful analysis? Is the question well-defined enough to produce concrete, testable predictions?

Impact and novelty

Does resolving this question meaningfully advance the field? Is the current literature genuinely inadequate? Is there unmet need?

Validation feasibility

Can the PI — or anyone — realistically perform wet-lab validation within an academic-scale budget?

Marvin's comparative advantage

Does an automated integration system genuinely add value over a postdoc with standard tools? If not, we'll say so and suggest you skip us.

Portfolio diversity

Each cohort balances across disease areas, data modalities, institution types, and career stages. Good science shouldn't require being at a well-funded R1.

Every rejected submission receives specific, written feedback and an invitation to resubmit. We do not ghost.

Deliverable tiers

Final tier is assigned by the selection committee based on data availability, scope, and PI engagement.

Tier 1 Literature synthesis + hypothesis generation 2–4 weeks

Structured literature review, dataset identification, gap analysis, and generated hypotheses. Appropriate when data availability is limited but the question is strong enough to warrant a structured framing.

Tier 2 · Default Computational analysis on public data 6–10 weeks

Everything in Tier 1, plus systematic integrated analysis across public datasets, ML-based target or biomarker identification, reproducible pipeline. Delivered as preprint-ready manuscript draft + analysis notebooks + hypothesis file.

Tier 3 Full pipeline with living updates 8–12 weeks + ongoing

Everything in Tier 2, plus ongoing monitoring for new data and publications, periodic re-analysis, and a living dashboard. Reserved for high-impact projects with strong PI engagement for a 6-month updates window.

Openness

Open by default. The line is outputs vs. engine: everything Marvin produces is open; how Marvin works internally is commercial.

Open

  • Research results and findings
  • Computational pipelines and code (Apache 2.0)
  • Text, figures, and preprints (CC-BY 4.0)
  • Data source manifests & accessions
  • Raw intermediate outputs
  • Selection criteria & cohort decisions
  • Project status dashboards

Not open

  • Marvin's core architecture & training methodology
  • Internal review deliberations (outputs are public; process is not)
  • Submission details for unselected projects

Publishing the full computational pipelines means anyone can reproduce the analysis with their own tools, independent of Marvin. That's the key credibility signal — and the reason we think this boundary is defensible.

IP & authorship · collaboration agreement

Iluvatar takes no IP on findings produced through the initiative. If you or third parties choose to pursue IP on downstream work, that's your prerogative. The one-page collaboration agreement for selected PIs is intentionally plain-language:

Iluvatar commits to

  • Delivering the agreed analysis tier on the stated timeline
  • Publishing all findings openly (preprint + GitHub + hypothesis file)
  • Not claiming IP on any findings
  • Crediting the submitting PI as lead collaborator via CRediT

You commit to

  • Providing written feedback within 30 days (best effort)
  • Allowing Iluvatar to publicly describe the collaboration
  • Acknowledging Marvin & Iluvatar in resulting publications
  • Sharing wet-lab validation results if performed (strongly encouraged, not required — a negative validation is a valuable output)

Authorship via CRediT

We use the CRediT Contributor Roles Taxonomy. Submitting PI gets Conceptualization and Supervision plus any roles actively filled. Iluvatar team gets Methodology, Software, Formal Analysis, Data Curation, Visualization. Community contributors get credit for specific roles based on actual contributions. Marvin is acknowledged in the methods section, not listed as an author — we don't want the AI-authorship debate distracting from the work.

Contributing

All contributions happen on GitHub. The website is the reading layer; GitHub is where work happens. Four paths by commitment level:

Comment on a hypothesis · ~5 min

Leave a scientific comment on any formalized hypothesis in any active project. Browse active projects →

Suggest a missing paper or dataset · ~15 min

File a structured issue on the relevant project repo. Marvin considers suggestions for the next scheduled re-analysis.

Review an analysis · 1–2 hours

Inspect the pipeline, question methodology, challenge parameter choices, or propose alternative normalization. All pipelines are Docker-reproducible.

Validate a hypothesis · weeks

Pick up a testable prediction if you have the experimental system. Protocols are in the project repo. Co-authorship per CRediT.
